[[1750-1900]] -- intellectual cause of [[unit 5]]
an intellectual movement that applied new ways of understanding, being rational basically
happens after [[europe]] scientific revolution (newton, galileo, descartes) which questioned [[monotheistic]] religious authority

core ideas:
	individualism -- the most basic element of society is an individual, not a group
	natural rights -- people are born with rights (life, liberty, property)
	social contract -- govt's authority comes from consent of the governed
	separation of powers
	deism -- god as a clockmaker, not actively intervening
	progress -- humanity can be improved through reason

key thinkers (memorize these for FRQs):
	thomas hobbes -- leviathan
		humans are naturally selfish + violent
		life without government = "nasty, brutish, and short"
		people surrender freedoms to a strong ruler -> ABSOLUTISM
		direct intellectual support for [[absolutism]] (france, russia)
	john locke -- natural rights (life, liberty, property), consent of governed
		two treatises of government
		humans naturally rational + cooperative
		if government fails to protect rights -> people can OVERTHROW it (fuels [[revoultions]])
		also wrote essay concerning human understanding -- humans born as TABULA RASA (blank slate)
		intelligence shaped by environment -> challenges hereditary privilege + aristocracy
		BIG influence on american revolution (jefferson lifted from him)
	jean-jacques rousseau -- social contract, "general will"
		BIG influence on french revolution
	voltaire -- religious tolerance, free speech, attacked church
	montesquieu -- separation of powers (legislative/executive/judicial)
		BIG influence on us constitution
	adam smith -- "wealth of nations" 1776, free markets, invisible hand
		founder of liberalism + capitalism
	mary wollstonecraft -- "vindication of rights of woman" 1792, early feminism
	olympe de gouges -- "declaration of rights of woman" french version

consequences:
	[[revoultions]] (american, french, haitian, latin american)
	abolition movements
	end of serfdom
	women's suffrage
	rise of nationalism
	constitutional governments

salons -- intellectual gatherings (often hosted by women) where ideas spread
print culture -- newspapers, pamphlets, encyclopedia (diderot)
enlightened despots -- rulers who adopted enlightenment ideas only when it strengthened their power
	frederick ii of prussia (see [[absolutism]])
	catherine the great ([[russia]])
	maria theresa (austria)

DEISM -- enlightenment-era religious view:
	major voice: thomas paine, the age of reason
	god created the world but does not interfere ("watchmaker analogy")
	rejects organized religion's authority
	shift from church dominance to rational religion

direct successful enlightenment revolution = AMERICAN REVOLUTION
	declaration of independence (jefferson) -- heavy locke influence
	us constitution implements enlightenment thinkers:
		locke -> natural rights, limited government
		montesquieu -> separation of powers
		rousseau -> popular sovereignty
		voltaire -> civil liberties
		smith -> economic freedom
	first successful enlightenment-based revolution

big-picture shifts:
	political: divine right -> popular sovereignty
	economic: mercantilism -> capitalism
	social: hierarchy by birth -> rights by nature
	intellectual: faith authority -> reason authority
